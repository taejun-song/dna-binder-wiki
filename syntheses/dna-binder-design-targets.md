# DNA Binder Design Targets for Genome Editing

A survey of the 9 genomic DNA target sequences for which [[rfdiffusion3]] binders have been designed, spanning disease-relevant loci, transcription factor binding sites, and repetitive elements.

## Target overview

| Shorthand | DNA target sequence | Biological context | Designs |
|---|---|---|---|
| PNRP1 | TGAGGAGAGGAG | Prion protein (PRNP) locus | 38 |
| CAG | CAGCAGCAGCAG | CAG trinucleotide repeat (Huntington's, spinocerebellar ataxias) | 14 |
| HSTELO | AGGGTTAGGGTT | Human telomeric repeat (TTAGGG)₂ | 13 |
| OCT4pt2 | GGGCTTGCGA | OCT4 / POU5F1 promoter region (part 2) | 9 |
| OCT4pt1 | GGTGAAATGA | OCT4 / POU5F1 promoter region (part 1) | 7 |
| NFKB | GGGGATTCCCCC | NF-κB consensus binding site | 6 |
| HD | GCTTAATTAGCG | Homeodomain binding site | 2 |
| TATA | CGTATAAACG | TATA box (core promoter element) | 1 |
| Dux4grna2 | CAGGCCGCAGG | DUX4 guide RNA target (facioscapulohumeral dystrophy) | 1 |

Total: 92 designs across all targets.

## Design strategy

All binders were generated by [[rfdiffusion3]] using its DNA-binding protein design pipeline:

1. Diffuse protein structure conditioned on a fixed DNA target conformation (predicted by [[alphafold3]]).
2. Assign sequences using [[ligandmpnn]].
3. For promising backbones, re-scaffold the major-groove recognition region using a "specblock" protocol to increase diversity (indicated by `_specblock_` in design names).

Protein lengths range from ~110 to ~145 residues. Both initial designs and specblock re-scaffolded variants are included in the dataset.

## Biological relevance

The targets were chosen to cover diverse genome editing applications:

- **Disease genes**: PRNP (prion diseases), CAG repeats (polyglutamine expansion disorders), DUX4 (FSHD)
- **Transcription factor sites**: OCT4 (reprogramming/pluripotency), NF-κB (immune signaling), homeodomain (developmental regulation)
- **Structural elements**: telomeric repeats (chromosome maintenance), TATA box (transcription initiation)

Programmable DNA-binding proteins that recognize these sequences without requiring guide RNAs could complement CRISPR-based approaches for therapeutic genome editing.
